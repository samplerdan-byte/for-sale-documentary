13.8 BILLION YEARS AGO

Act I: In the Beginning

Before corruption, before money, before life — there was only energy looking for structure.

In the beginning, there was nothing. Then there was everything.

13.8 billion years ago, all matter and energy in the universe existed in a point smaller than an atom. It exploded. Hydrogen formed. Gravity pulled it together. Stars ignited. Stars died. Their deaths created heavier elements — carbon, oxygen, iron. Those elements formed planets. On one planet, chemistry became biology.

For 4 billion years, life was simple. Single cells. Then multicellular organisms. Then something remarkable: competition. The moment two organisms wanted the same resource, the game began.

The rules were set by physics: energy flows toward whoever can capture and concentrate it most efficiently. Stars do it with gravity. Cells do it with membranes. Empires do it with armies. Corporations do it with lobbyists. The mechanism changes. The law never does.

The universe doesn't care about fairness. It rewards concentration of power. Everything that follows is just that law playing out, faster and faster, with increasingly clever tools.

252 MYA – 1945 CE

Act II: Dominance

Every dominant species believes its reign is permanent. None of them are right.

252 MILLION YEARS AGO
The Great Dying
96% of all species wiped out. The Permian extinction cleared the board. What filled the vacuum? Dinosaurs — the most successful large animals in Earth's history. They dominated for 165 million years. Unchallenged. Unchallengeable.
66 MILLION YEARS AGO
The Asteroid
A 7.5-mile rock hit the Yucatán at 45,000 mph. 165 million years of dominance ended in a day. The lesson: no amount of power protects you from a force you didn't see coming. Small mammals survived in the margins. They inherited everything.
~300,000 YEARS AGO
Homo Sapiens
Not the strongest, fastest, or largest. But the species that could tell stories. Language let humans coordinate at scale. Myths, religions, laws, currencies — all stories that let strangers cooperate. The tool changed everything.
~3000 BCE
The Pyramids
The first megastructures, built by centralized power extracting labor and resources. The pharaohs didn't just rule — they controlled the economy, the religion, and the narrative. The original too-big-to-fail. The original story that justified taking from the many to enrich the few.
753 BCE – 476 CE
Rome
The greatest empire in the ancient world. At its peak, 70 million people under one government. The legions conquered with iron. The Senate was purchased with gold. Senators took bribes from provincial governors. Generals bought armies with treasury money. Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon because the Senate tried to prosecute him for corruption. The empire fell when the currency was debased, the military was privatized, and the barbarians at the gates were better organized than the bureaucrats inside them.
1095–1291
The Crusades
God wills it. The Pope promised salvation to anyone who fought. Kings promised land. Merchants financed the wars and took the trade routes. Venice and Genoa became the first financial superpowers by funding holy wars. The Knights Templar invented international banking — depositing money in one country, withdrawing in another. They became so wealthy that the King of France arrested them all on a Friday the 13th and seized their assets. The first hostile takeover of a bank.
1337–1453
The Hundred Years' War
England versus France. 116 years of war over who owns what. The real innovation: permanent taxation. Before the Hundred Years' War, kings asked for money when they needed it. After it, taxes became permanent. War taught governments that once you start taxing, you never have to stop.
1618–1648
The Thirty Years' War
Started as a religious war. Became a political war. Eight million dead — the most destructive European conflict before the 20th century. The Peace of Westphalia created the modern nation-state system. The lesson: every war starts with an ideology and ends with a treaty about money and territory.
1637
Tulip Mania
A single tulip bulb traded for more than a house in Amsterdam. The first recorded speculative bubble. When it popped, the Dutch government refused to enforce futures contracts. Sound familiar?
1720
The South Sea Bubble
The South Sea Company bribed members of Parliament with stock to secure a monopoly on trade. Stock went from £100 to £1,000 in months, then collapsed. MPs who took the bribes? Most kept their seats. Isaac Newton lost £20,000.
1920
Charles Ponzi
Promised 50% returns in 45 days. Paid early investors with new investors' money. The scheme collapsed in months but the name stuck forever. Every crypto fraud since follows the same blueprint.
1914–1918
World War I
20 million dead. The war that ended empires: Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German. Arms manufacturers — Krupp, Vickers, Schneider — sold weapons to both sides before the war started. J.P. Morgan lent $2.3 billion to the Allies (over $50 billion in today's dollars). The Treaty of Versailles imposed $33 billion in reparations on Germany. The seeds of the next war were planted in the peace treaty of the last one.
1929
The Great Depression
The stock market lost 89% of its value. 15 million Americans unemployed. Banks failed by the thousands. Congress passed Glass-Steagall to separate commercial and investment banking. It held for 66 years — until Wall Street spent $300 million lobbying to repeal it in 1999. Nine years later, the financial system collapsed again.
1939–1945
World War II
75 million dead. The deadliest conflict in human history. IBM sold punch card systems to Nazi Germany. Standard Oil sold fuel additives. Ford built trucks. Coca-Cola invented Fanta for the German market. After the war, not one American corporation was prosecuted for trading with the enemy. Operation Paperclip recruited 1,600 Nazi scientists to work for the United States. The bomb that ended the war was built with $2 billion in taxpayer money by the same government that hired the scientists who helped start it.
1945
The Computer
ENIAC. The first general-purpose electronic computer. 30 tons, 18,000 vacuum tubes. Built for calculating artillery trajectories. The U.S. military funded it; the military always funds it first. Within a decade, computers would move from government labs to corporations to, eventually, every pocket on Earth.
1947–1991
The Cold War
The arms race. Two superpowers spend $10 trillion building weapons capable of ending civilization. The military-industrial complex Eisenhower warned about becomes permanent. Defense contractors — Lockheed, Boeing, Raytheon — become some of the most powerful corporations on Earth. Every technological breakthrough of the 20th century — the internet, GPS, microchips, satellites — was first funded by military money.
1955–1975
Vietnam
58,220 Americans dead. 2 million Vietnamese dead. The Pentagon Papers revealed the government lied about the war for years. Defense contractors' profits doubled. Agent Orange was manufactured by Dow Chemical and Monsanto. Decades later, both companies are still in business. The soldiers who fought the war got Agent Orange. The companies that made it got contracts.
1956
AI — The First Promise
The Dartmouth Conference. John McCarthy coins "artificial intelligence." Researchers predict human-level AI within 20 years. Government funding pours in. The first AI hype cycle begins.
1958
The Microchip
Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments and Robert Noyce at Fairchild Semiconductor independently invent the integrated circuit. A single chip replaces thousands of individual components. Noyce later co-founds Intel. Moore's Law begins: transistor density doubles every two years. The exponential curve that will reshape civilization.
1974–1993
AI Winter
The promises don't deliver. Government funding dries up. The British Lighthill Report calls AI research a failure. DARPA cuts funding. Two decades of "AI Winter" follow. The pattern repeats: hype, funding, disappointment, collapse, repeat. Sound familiar?
1971–1990s
Silicon Valley
Intel's 4004 — the first commercial microprocessor. Four bits. 2,300 transistors. By 1993, the Pentium had 3.1 million. A semiconductor company founded in a garage becomes the center of the global economy. The Valley's culture: build fast, monetize faster, regulate never.
1990s
The Internet & MySpace
The internet was supposed to democratize information. For a brief moment, it did. GeoCities, MySpace, early forums — anyone could publish, anyone could connect. Then the platforms realized the real product was attention, and the real business was selling it.
2012–2023

Act III: The Promise

"A purely peer-to-peer version of electronic cash would allow online payments to be sent directly from one party to another without going through a financial institution."

On October 31, 2008 — six weeks after Lehman Brothers collapsed — a pseudonymous figure called Satoshi Nakamoto published the Bitcoin whitepaper. The pitch was simple: money without banks, transactions without trust, a financial system that couldn't be corrupted because no one controlled it.

For a while, the dream held. Early Bitcoin was used by cypherpunks, libertarians, and people in countries with failed currencies. It was weird, idealistic, and mostly harmless.

Then the money showed up.

By 2017, Bitcoin hit $20,000. By 2021, $69,000. Total crypto market cap peaked at $3 trillion. And with the money came exactly the same people the technology was supposed to make obsolete: the fraudsters, the lobbyists, the politicians for hire.

The tool built to fight corruption became the most efficient corruption machine ever created.

AI Across the Decades

Every generation promises artificial intelligence will change everything. Every generation is right — just not in the way they promised.

1950
Turing's Question
Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." The first line: "Can machines think?" He proposes the Imitation Game — later called the Turing Test. The question that will haunt the next 75 years.
1956
The Dartmouth Conference
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester gather for a summer workshop. They coin "artificial intelligence" and predict: "Every aspect of learning can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." They request two months and ten researchers. It will take 67 years and trillions of dollars.
1958–1969
The Golden Age
The Perceptron — the first neural network. ELIZA — the first chatbot (a therapist that just repeated your questions back). SHAKEY — the first mobile robot. DARPA pours millions in. Minsky tells Life Magazine: "Within a generation, the problem of creating artificial intelligence will be substantially solved." It isn't.
1974–1980
First AI Winter
The Lighthill Report in the UK calls AI research a failure. DARPA funding collapses. Minsky and Papert publish Perceptrons, mathematically proving neural networks can't solve XOR. An entire field goes dormant. Thousands of researchers move on.
1980–1987
Expert Systems Boom
Japan announces the Fifth Generation Computer Project: $850 million to build thinking machines. The US panics. Corporations spend $1 billion/year on "expert systems" — rule-based AI. The Lisp machine market hits $400 million. The hype cycle repeats.
1987–1993
Second AI Winter
Expert systems can't handle real-world complexity. The Lisp machine market collapses overnight. Japan's Fifth Generation Project fails to meet a single goal. DARPA's new director cuts AI funding and says: "AI will not produce the next generation of weapons systems." Researchers learn to never use the words "artificial intelligence" in grant proposals.
1997
Deep Blue Beats Kasparov
IBM's chess machine defeats the world champion. Kasparov accuses IBM of cheating. He was wrong, but his instinct was right: the machine didn't think like he did. It just calculated faster. IBM's stock rises $18 billion. The real winner was always the shareholders.
2012
The Deep Learning Revolution
AlexNet, a neural network running on video game GPUs, crushes the ImageNet competition by 10 percentage points. The technique — deep learning — had been around since the 1980s. What changed? Data (the internet) and compute (Moore's Law). Google, Facebook, and Amazon realize they can predict human behavior. The AI gold rush begins.
2016
AlphaGo
DeepMind's AI beats the world Go champion, Lee Sedol. Move 37 in Game 2 is a play no human would make — and it wins. Sedol says: "I thought AlphaGo was based on probability calculation and that it was merely a machine. But when I saw this move, I changed my mind." He retires from professional Go three years later.
NOV 2022
ChatGPT
100 million users in two months. The fastest-growing consumer application in history. OpenAI, a nonprofit founded to ensure AI benefits all humanity, pivots to a capped-profit structure, then tries to go fully for-profit. Sam Altman lobbies Congress for AI regulation — regulation that, conveniently, only the biggest companies can afford to comply with.
2021
The AI Schism
Dario Amodei, VP of Research at OpenAI, leaves with a dozen top researchers over disagreements about safety and commercialization. They found Anthropic — the "safety-first" AI lab. OpenAI had started as a nonprofit to benefit all of humanity. Then it created a capped-profit arm. Then it tried to go fully for-profit. Anthropic says safety-first but raises $7.6 billion from Google, Amazon, and others. Every idealistic tech venture follows the same arc: start with a mission, then the money shows up, and the mission bends toward the money. The pattern is older than the technology. It's older than electricity. It's older than money itself.
